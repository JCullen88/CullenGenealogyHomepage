Probate Inventory of Richard Cullen
Husbandman of Upton 1580
Jim Cullen

Transcribed below is the 1580 Probate Inventory of Richard Cullen, husbandman, of Upton, Nottinghamshire. The exact date of the original document can not be made out clearly due to the age and the wrinkling of the parchment which can not be remedied due to the high risk of further damage. The month February is very clear though. According to the abstract, the date was February 19, 1579 on the old calendar - or 1580 on the new calendar. I want to thank the Nottinghamshire Archives for their kindness, their professionalism, and their efficient work in making these documents available. This particular document is PR/SW/13/7B which is the Probate Inventory legally mandated at the time of death in order for the will to be proved. An itemized list of all the deceased's movable belongings and their value was to be appraised and attached. Within this was found also PR/SW/13/7A which was the original will itself, something amazing to see since I had never laid eyes on it before. Nearly forty percent of the original document has been eaten away by age and about a third of what remains is barely legible. Luckily there is the Probate Copy held by the local eclesiastical court, the Court of the Peculiar of the Chapter of the Collegiate Church of Southwell or simply the Peculiar Court of Southwell. The entire text of Richard's will was taken from the Probate Copy. Comparing the portions of the original that survive to the Probate Copy, I find the match to be exact.

One thing that became abundantly clear as soon as I started to skim over the contents of the inventory was that our prior image of Richard being more of a simple farmer leading a rough life with little in the way of niceties - that idea needed some mending. Richard's wealth was striking and was a bit surprising at first. The value of his movable goods alone was extraordinary. There was brass, pewter, furniture, linens, implements throughout the house, equipment of many varieties. Richard was running what must have been a very diverse and profitable operation in Upton. Recall from his will that he rented out three houses besides the one he lived in! Add to this the fact that Richard died at a relatively young age. I doubt he would have had the time to build that kind of fortune on his own. For these reasons I've determined that finding out more about Richard's father, who is also buried in Upton, is more of a priority now. Until then, we can read through Richard's 1580 Probate Inventory to get a better idea of what his life must have been like.

We see the inventory being carried out by Thomas Bartherone, Richard Lambe, John Plowman, and George Bykeark. John Plowman and and Richard Lambe were also signed witnesses to the 1580 will of Richard Cullen so they must have been close to the Cullen family. The other witness was William Gill, a name found also on the 1588 Executor's Account, along with Richard Lambe and Thomas Barterome. Richard Cullen would have been very familiar with the four men who performed this inventory. By law they have to have been upstanding and trusted friends or neighbors. Usually they were not family or anyone who had financial interests in the finances of the estate they were taking inventory of. These men were very likely neighbors who were familiar with Richard's farm operations and with the value of the crops and equipment found on the property.

We'll take note of the scale, the cost, and the risk of Richard's massive farm operation. By taking into account crop yields per acre, the time of year the inventory was carried out, and the items in the inventory, we can estimate that Richard's farm operation likely occupied nearly 100 acres, especially if we take into account the three houses that he rented out to other families in the Village. These would occupy 4 acres each on their own. Now 33 acres is approximately a yardland or oxgang, that amount of land required to be self-sufficient and to pay your rent. Having two yardlands was the mark of ver profitable and successful farming operation. Richard occupied THREE yardlands which placed him easily within the top 4% of profitable farm operations in Upton. Given there were about 80 families in the Village at the time, that places the Cullen family within the top three profitable families. Richard also used horses for his plowing at a time when oxen were the norm. Oxen were powerful, affordable, and if your oxen died you could reclaim part of your investment by selling the meat. Horses were expensive to feed, maintain, and care for. If your horse died, your investment disappeared. However, horses were fast. When you had 100 acres to plough and get planted, if you could afford horses then that was the way to go. Richard could easily afford the investment and it was well worth the risk.

It's also interesting that Richard only farmed the minimum amount of wheat, probably just enough to provide his own family's needs for the season. Wheat was very profitable but was expensive to farm and was a volatile crop. Instead, Richard pivoted and leaned towards barley and peas. Richard had £8 of barley in the lathe (stored in the barn) which is an incredible amount on its own. There was also another £6 in the ground to be sown and another £2-1/2 of barley rye mix in another barn. Richard didn't simply sell the grain, he ran his own malting operation which could easily double or triple his profit margin. By malting his own barley and brewing his own ale, Richard could provide high-value processed products to the local markets. He had six quarters of malt in his inventory, worth over £3 at market. Richard also made a massive investment in field pease (using the old spelling), which was incredibly profitable. Field pease could feed livestock through the winter, be sold as a staple food product, or be used in crop rotation to fix nitrogen back into the soil. Pease that he had already sown into the ground was appraised at an incredible £12 and another £1 plus unthreshed.
