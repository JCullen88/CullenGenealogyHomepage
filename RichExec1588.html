Executorship Account and Inventory of Richard Cullen
Husbandman of Upton 1588
Jim Cullen

This is the transcription of the Executorship Account and Inventory of Richard Cullen, Husbandman of Upton, Nottinghamshire, from October 25, 1588. This has been one of the most interesting documents I've come across in researching the early Cullen families of Upton. I believe that this Executor's Account was triggered by the eldest son Thomas Cullen (Yeoman and Churchwarden of Upton) attaining the age of 21. The 14 year term that Richard Cullen determined for his wife Agnes to hold the farm to raise his children on would end in 1594. At that point, Thomas would take over ownership. In 1588 though Thomas turned 21 meaning that he could ask for his share, or 'children's portion' of the estate. This triggered the Executor's Account and Inventory. This document is an inventory of the items sold to raise the children's portion. Richard's wife Agnes, also receives a share by law.

The members of the Cullen family mentioned in the Account are: Richard Cullen (deceased), Agnes Cullen (widow), Catherne Cullen (daughter Katherine), Margarie Cullen (daughter Margery), and Elizabeth Cullen. Elizabeth Cullen was a surprise since no Elizabeth was known in the family yet, here she was, taking items from the estate. She is mentioned twice and both times she is together with Margery Cullen. They took about 2 pounds 13 shillings worth of household items: one coverlet, one mattress, one bolster, 4 pillowbers (pillowcases), 2 window-cloths (curtains or decorative hangings), one great pan, one pot, one spit (for roasting), and 2 chests. I've come to the conclusion that Elizabeth was the youngest child of Richard and Agnes. When Richard died in 1580 he was still relatively young at about 45 years of age. This daughter would be the last of nine children: Katherine, Margery, Thomas, Jennett, William, Alice, Jonye, Margaret, and Elizabeth. Two of his children had to be over the age of 17 in 1580 in order for them to be sworn in as Executors so that means nine children were born between about 1560 and 1580 which is a tight squeeze! When Richard died in 1580, it's no surprise that Agnes at that time was pregnant or had just given birth. Elizabeth was not mentioned in Richard's will in 1580 either because she had not been born or because she was a young infant. Richard's will would cover a child of his born in this circumstance. In 1588 then, Elizabeth was about 8 and her sister was about 26. With Agnes running the farm and raising the children on her own, it makes sense that an older, more established sibling, would share this load by taking in a younger sibling.

The list of names besides the Cullens in the Executor's Account are equally fascinating. They are the neighbors, friends, and peers of the Cullens in the Village of Upton in the 1580's. Many of these surnames had been found in Upton for generations and would continue to be found in the Village for generations to come. I've pulled the names from the text and repeated them here: Henry Pryd, William Pryd, William Gill, Robert Heifeld, William Pinkes, Thomas Kitching, George Bykirke, G John Lawe, Thomas Barterome, Richard Lambe, William Harrisonne, Richard Baker, Thomas Waddington, Thomas Yearwod, Henrye Watsone, Richard Morsby, Mr. Stennsone, Adam Story, John Raulston, Robert Moke, Thomas Calleife, Robert Gibson, James Foreste, [S]ayth Kitching, Mrs. Gylfhurste, William Fosteard, and Richard Moslay. William and Henry Pryd are of special interest since they were 'Executors of Trust' and so would have been highly respected and trusted individuals. They could have been brothers-in-law, maternal uncles, or the absolute most trusted lifelong friends. Comparing this list to the list of names on the 1642 Protestation Return, including the Cullens, we find that better than 40% of the surnames or phonetic equivalents are still present in Upton two generations later. This is remarkable and is an indication of how tight-knit these communities were. This figure would probably be higher if we deduct those names of people who lived in villages other than Upton who came for Richard's estate sale.

We can run this same comparison on the list of surnames found in the marriage registers of Upton at the time. This is casting a wider net of course but represents the surnames from all socia-ecnomic classes that were involved in and around Upton from roughly 1560 to 1600. Taking from Phillimore's we find: Addenbroughe, Ash, Aucocke, Ausley, Ausleye, Baie, Banes, Barber, Bartram, Beane, Beckingham, Benett, Burnam, Caleye, Clarke, Coapelande, Cooke, Cooper, Cullen, Danks, Fenton, Flower, Forrest, Gibson, Gill, Gyles, Hanson, Harper, Holmes, Hoyes, Huddleston, Huggett, Keye, Kirkbee, Kitchen, Kyrke, Lambe, Lowe, Lownde, Martiall, Medopp, Morcroft, Paine, Pryde, Rawson, Robinson, Sandame, Saunsome, Savage, Savige, Smithe, Smythe, Stainton, Swyft, Watts, West, Whitakers, Wilson, Woodsend, Woorsley, Woorsleye. This time we find that just over 26% of the surnames in the 1560-1600 marriage registers are found also in the 1642 Protestation Returns. I take this fact to show that the people who came to Richard's estate sale were of a more specific group. They were families that represent the core group of peers of the Cullens that had long term ties to Upton and that we should make special note of them.
