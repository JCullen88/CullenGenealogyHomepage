Protestation Returns
Upton, Nottinghamshire 1642
Jim Cullen

This is a transcription of the 1642 Protestation Return for Upton, Nottinghamshire in 1642. Due to tensions between King Charles I and Parliament, an oath or Protestation was drafted, requiring every adult male over the age of 18 in Great Britain and Wales to maintain and defend the true Reformed Protestant Religion. The order was sent throughout the kingdom to the local officials (ministers, churchwardens, constables) and the men of every perish would gather, usually at the local church, to swear the oath and have their names recorded in a list. Those who refused to sign (recussant) were recorded in a separate list, identifying the dissenters in the parish community. Since this was to include every male over 18 in the kingdom or about 30% of the population, the Protestation Returns are considered one of the best census substitues between the Lay Subsidies and the Census of 1841. Of course I was interested in extracting information on the members of the Cullen family that might be included in the return but I found the list of surnames so fascinating that I decided to transcribe the entire document for the benefit of whoever may be interested. There are 109 entries in the list with four of them being Cullens. A few of the names on the list still contain question marks in the place of uncertain characters in the original document. Work resolving these difficulties continues but I have a very good resource as a guide. I have a copy of A Nottinghamshire Village in War and Peace: The Accounts of the Constables of Upton 1640-1666 edited by Martyn Bennett and published by the Thoroton Society of Nottinghamshire. I want to thank the Thoroton Society here for their work in transcribing the Constables Accounts from the original documents. Information derived from this source is referenced below by appearing within {curly braces}. Many of the same names and, I suspect, the very individuals in the list below appear in the book. Examples that I just now looked up include: Henry Roades, John Nutt, and Salomen Bettinson.
